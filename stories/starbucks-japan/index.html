The Corporate
X-Ray of Japan

2,108 Starbucks stores are spread across this country. The map of where they sit tells a story that has nothing to do with coffee.

By Kohei · April 2026
Scroll
↓

Starbucks doesn't open stores on instinct. The company runs demographic models, foot-traffic analyses, and competitive assessments before signing a lease. Each green logo pinned to a shopping strip or station concourse is, in a sense, a corporate vote of economic confidence — a bet that the surrounding population has the income, the habits, and the density to sustain it.

Japan was one of Starbucks' earliest international expansions. The first Tokyo store opened in 1996. Three decades later, there are 2,108 locations spread across all 47 prefectures. That's more than the United Kingdom, more than South Korea, and roughly half the number you'd find in China.

But the map of where those stores sit is more than a consumer preference survey. It's a fine-grained snapshot of where economic energy flows in Japan — which cities are growing, which are contracting, and which surprising pockets of prosperity exist where you'd least expect them. 06 / Focus

Zoom into Fukuoka.

The gateway to Kyushu, Japan's southernmost main island. Prefecture-wide, Fukuoka ranks 8th by per-capita density — unremarkable on the national stage. But zoom in to the municipality level, and a striking contrast appears.

07 / Contrast

Two cities. One diverging story.

In southern Fukuoka: Fukuoka City — 52 stores, 1.6M residents, 3.23 per 100k.

In northern Fukuoka: Kitakyushu — 13 stores, 913k residents, 1.42 per 100k. Below the national average. The same prefecture; a very different economic trajectory.

The Fukuoka Divide

A rivalry that was, and what remains

Fifty years ago, these two cities were near-equals in ambition and economic weight. Today, the Starbucks map traces the outline of their diverging fates.

Kitakyushu once anchored Japan's postwar industrial recovery. Its steel mills, chemical plants, and port facilities made it a symbol of national resurgence — at its peak in the 1960s, it was producing more steel than the entire United Kingdom. The city was designated Japan's first "environmental model city" after its legendary battle to clean up its own toxic air.

Fukuoka City, its neighbor 60 kilometers to the south, was historically quieter — a commercial port, a gateway to Korea and China, a university town. But something shifted. As heavy industry declined globally, Fukuoka leaned into what it had: geography, young population, and an urban core dense enough to generate its own momentum.

Fukuoka City is now Japan's fastest-growing major city by population. Kitakyushu has lost roughly 200,000 residents since its 1979 peak. The Starbucks map didn't create this story — it just renders it visible.

Fukuoka's startup ecosystem now rivals Tokyo's in energy, if not in scale. The city has attracted international tech companies, young migrants from across Kyushu, and a thriving food and creative culture. Starbucks followed: 52 locations, including a flagship Reserve Roastery. Corporate site selection isn't charity — it goes where the spending power is.

What Starbucks knows

Starbucks' site selection methodology is, at its core, a bet on where the middle class will be in five to ten years. The company isn't just measuring today's foot traffic; it's modeling demographic trajectories, income distributions, and the subtle urban qualities — walkability, young-adult density, international character — that predict consumer behavior.

In this sense, the Japan Starbucks map is a kind of revealed preference study of a major corporation's read on Japanese urban vitality. It won't always be right. Outliers exist — single highway-exit locations that serve a regional population but sit within a small municipality, inflating its per-capita figure. Small towns in Shiga, Tottori, and Yamanashi appear disproportionately high in the rankings for exactly this reason.

But at scale, the pattern holds. The prefectures and cities that score high on Starbucks density are, almost without exception, places that demographers and economists already identify as winners in Japan's ongoing urbanization: Tokyo's core wards, Fukuoka City, Okinawa's tourist corridors, Aichi's manufacturing belt.

The green logos aren't just cafés. They're data points in a decades-long story about which Japanese cities are gaining, and which are quietly being left behind.

Data & Methodology

Store data was collected from the official Starbucks Japan store locator in April 2026. The dataset covers 2,108 unique store URLs across all 47 prefectures. Stores were geocoded to the municipality level using Japanese administrative codes (N03_007).

Population figures are from Japan's 2020 National Census as published by the Statistics Bureau of Japan (e-Stat). Per-capita figures use total registered population.

Municipality boundaries are derived from the Geospatial Information Authority of Japan (国土地理院) administrative boundary data, 2025 edition. Boundaries were dissolved to the municipality level and simplified for web rendering.
